Wali : Hands-Free Baby Gate

2.009 Product

  • Date: Fall 2014

Wali is a baby gate with sensors to detect whether an object that is approaching the gate is an adult or not, then safely unlocks to protect children, but allow ease of use by adults. I mainly worked on the electronics and sensors but as a team of 24 students and several mentors, we all worked together to develop the product during the semester. The pictures below show a few iterations and the barebones board I designed.

Skyline Yoyos

2.008 Project

  • Date: Fall 2014

Click here to see the team's blog for a detailed explanation of the project. A team of 6 students made 50 yoyos to tight tolerances. There were 4 injection molded parts and 1 thermoformed part which snap-fit together in each yoyo. Inspired by the Brass Rat, on one side is the Boston skyline; the Cambridge skyline is on the other. Each skyline rotates on a bearing so it can stay still even as the yoyo spins.

Autonomous Robot Hall-Effect Scanner

2.007: Design & Manufacturing

The theme of the 2013 competition was Operation, the board game. Each round consisted of a head-to-head match between two robots which tried to score as many points as possible. The first 30 seconds of each round were autonomous, then students could use remote controls during the final 90 seconds.

My robot's task was to read and report the beaver's brain activity. Under the game board's surface was an array of electromagnetic solenoids, placed in the shape of a 7-bit display. Before each round, a referee would randomly choose a letter or number to represent what the beaver was thinking. The corresponding solenoids were turned on or off, indicating one of 2, 0, 7, I, H, T, F, P.

I used Hall-effect sensors to measure the strength of the magnetic field near each solenoid, to decide whether each was on or off. Since we were limited to only 3 sensors, and I needed to measure 7 B-fields, I split the problem into three steps: drive over first 3 solenoids--stop and measure, drive over next 3--stop and measure, and drive over last one--stop and measure. Then with data on all 7 solenoids, I analyzed the information to determine which ones were on. Then if the array of on/off matched that of a potential correct reading, I displayed the answer. Rather than waste up to 7 of my limited number of digital pins to output a letter, I connected a servo with a pointer on it, with each 30 degree sector of a semicircle marked with a letter.

I used an Arduino microcontroller board to upload the code in Arduino's C++ -like language. The code handled the following decisions:

  • controlling the rotation speed of the wheels
  • reading several digital pins
  • deciding if a solenoid was on or off based on multiple readings
  • controlling a motor to display final answer
